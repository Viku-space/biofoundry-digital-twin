Overview

A building that runs biology by itself

The whole structure is one integrated robotic system: a stack of specialised laboratories, each a self-contained robotic cell, connected by an overhead transport network and directed by a central orchestration core. No humans work inside — they supervise everything remotely from a separate operations building through a digital twin. Because nobody is inside, the lab can seal itself completely.

Principle

Diversity at scale

Conventional biofoundries scale throughput — the same protocol, replicated. This scales diversity: many distinct environments running different biology in parallel, in one orchestrated building.

Autonomy

Fully robotic

Every instrument is driven by its cell controller and serviced by robots — a bench arm plus a 3-axis gantry per compartment. Nothing is human-operated.

Safety

Sealable

Material only crosses the envelope through airlocks. With no one inside, the building can lock down — nothing in, nothing out — in seconds.

Floors · logical reasoning

Why each floor is where it is

The stack isn't arbitrary. Reading top to bottom, it follows physics (vibration, air, light, heat), material logic (what feeds what), and safety (containment isolated up top, storage stable down low).

L4 · TOP

High-Containment

Compartments: Gowning/airlock, decon, BSL-3 Suites A/B, isolation.
Why here: containment goes at the very top with dedicated single-pass HVAC exhausting straight through the roof, so contaminated air never travels down through the building. It's an isolated loop — connected to everything else only through decon airlocks. This is where lockdown matters most.
Does: high-consequence pathogen work, fully contained.

L3

Specialised Cultivation

Compartments: anaerobic prep/chambers/ferment, photobioreactor hall, thermophiles, extreme ferment.
Why here: non-standard atmospheres (O₂-free), lighting (photobioreactors), and temperatures (extremophiles) each need dedicated infrastructure kept away from standard labs. Photobioreactors sit high for light access and to reject heat upward.
Does: specialised growth and bioproduction.

L2 · CENTRE

Molecular Biology — the foundry core

Compartments: automation foundry hall, strain foundry, cell culture ×2, media prep, screening.
Why here: the Design–Build–Test–Learn heart, placed centrally on purpose — one lift-hop from chemistry below (reagents) and cultivation above (growth). Most experiments spend most of their steps here, so it's positioned to minimise transport for the highest-traffic floor.
Does: builds and screens strains/constructs — the throughput engine.

L1 · GRADE

Chemistry & Synthesis

Compartments: reagent prep, synthesis bays, flow chemistry hall, glovebox/inert, chem store.
Why here: ground level is the loading dock — solvents, reagents and consumables enter here (heavy, ventilated, spill-contained). This floor manufactures the chemical building blocks that feed the biology above.
Does: produces reagents and small-molecule inputs; feeds upward.

▬ GRADE · GROUND LEVEL ▬
B1 · BELOW GRADE

Analytics

Compartments: NMR & Cryo-EM hall, LC-MS suite, prep & extraction, sample archive, data/QC.
Why below ground: the most vibration-sensitive instruments — NMR magnets, Cryo-EM columns — need isolated slabs away from mechanical noise, which is exactly what a basement gives. Analytics is the terminal step: samples come down to be measured, and results flow out as data.
Does: measurement and characterisation — consumes samples, produces data.

B2 · DEEPEST

Cold Storage & Environmental

Compartments: cryogenic biobank, cold rooms, environmental chambers, vibration-isolation lab, cryostore.
Why deepest: the most stable ground and the earth's thermal mass make this ideal for cold storage and ultra-sensitive work. This is the foundry's long-term memory — strains, samples and reagents banked and retrieved on demand.
Does: storage and environmental extremes; feeds material up.

Vertical flow logic

A typical experiment threads the stack: reagents (L1) → build & culture (L2) → specialised cultivation (L3) → analysis (B1), with storage (B2) buffering at both ends and containment (L4) running as an isolated loop. The overhead grid and lifts carry every item; the central orchestration core decides the schedule. The design goal is to keep the highest-traffic transitions on adjacent floors, because vertical lifts are the transport bottleneck — which is exactly what the optimisation on the next page formalises.

Flow Optimization · the mathematics

What the orchestration core is actually solving

“Optimising the flow” splits into two coupled problems: where to put each function (layout) and when to run each step and on which resource (scheduling). Both are formalised below; both are NP-hard, so the core runs exact solvers for small instances and heuristics / reinforcement learning at scale.

1 · Layout — a Quadratic Assignment Problem

Deciding which lab function lives on which floor is a QAP. Let \(\mathcal F\) be the set of functions and \(\mathcal L\) the floor slots. Let \(f_{ij}\) be the traffic (frequency of material transitions) between functions \(i\) and \(j\), taken from protocol statistics, and \(d_{kl}\) the transport cost between slots \(k\) and \(l\) — dominated by the number of lift hops \(|\text{floor}_k-\text{floor}_l|\). With binary \(x_{ik}=1\) if function \(i\) is placed at slot \(k\):

$$\min_{x}\;\sum_{i,j\in\mathcal F}\sum_{k,l\in\mathcal L} f_{ij}\,d_{kl}\,x_{ik}\,x_{jl} \quad\text{s.t.}\;\sum_{k}x_{ik}=1\;\forall i,\;\;\sum_{i}x_{ik}=1\;\forall k,\;\;x_{ik}\in\{0,1\}.$$

The minimiser co-locates high-traffic functions on adjacent floors — which is exactly why the DBTL core sits centrally on L2, reagents directly below on L1, and specialised cultivation directly above on L3.

2 · Scheduling — flexible job-shop with transport

Each experiment is a job \(j\in\mathcal J\) with an ordered chain of operations \(O_j=(o_{j1},\dots,o_{jn_j})\) (its protocol). Each operation \(o\) may run on any compatible compartment \(m\in\mathcal M_o\) with processing time \(p_{o,m}\); moving between compartments \(m\to m'\) costs transport time \(\tau_{m,m'}\) (Manhattan distance on the ceiling grid plus lift time). We choose an assignment \(a(o)\in\mathcal M_o\) and a start time \(s_o\):

$$\min\; C_{\max}=\max_{j\in\mathcal J} C_j,\qquad C_j = s_{o_{j n_j}} + p_{o_{j n_j},\,a(o_{j n_j})}$$

subject to

$$s_{o_{j,k+1}} \;\ge\; s_{o_{jk}} + p_{o_{jk},a(o_{jk})} + \tau_{a(o_{jk}),\,a(o_{j,k+1})}\quad\text{(precedence + transport)}$$
$$\text{no two operations overlap on the same compartment; capacity }c_m\text{ respected; BSL}(o)\le \text{BSL}(a(o)).$$

Alternative objectives the core can target: minimise total flow time \(\sum_j C_j\), or maximise throughput \(=|\mathcal J|/C_{\max}\) — the right metric for a diversity-scaling foundry.

3 · Throughput & utilisation

At steady state the building's output is bounded by how busy its compartments are. With utilisation \(\rho_m\) and cycle time \(T_m\) per compartment \(m\), effective throughput is

$$T_{\text{eff}} \;\approx\; \sum_{m\in\mathcal M}\frac{\rho_m}{T_m},\qquad \rho_m=\frac{\text{busy time}}{\text{total time}}\le 1.$$

By Little's Law the work-in-progress obeys \(L=\lambda\,W\) (WIP = arrival rate × mean cycle time), so the scheduler pushes \(\lambda\) up until a compartment hits \(\rho_m\!\to\!1\) — the bottleneck. Maximising \(T_{\text{eff}}\) therefore means two things the layout and scheduler jointly deliver: keep transport off the critical path (good QAP layout + routing) and balance load so no single compartment saturates while others idle.

Why this matters here

This is the formal version of the whole thesis: the building's value isn't running one protocol fast, it's running many different environments in parallel at high utilisation. The layout (QAP) and the online scheduler (FJSP-T) are what turn a stack of robotic labs into an optimised foundry — and they're exactly what the orchestration core in the 3-D model represents.
